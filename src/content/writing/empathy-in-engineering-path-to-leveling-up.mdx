I have been making products for a long time. I think it started with this
[WP Plugin](https://wordpress.org/plugins/wp-simple-event-management-system/) when I
needed a simple event management system for my
[college fest](https://www.intechgrity.com/wp-simple-event-management-plugin-by-itg-manage-college-fests-and-events/).

Then client of mine needed to create a basic and simple form on their website. After
delivering the form, I realized that I can also sell a
[more advanced version](https://codecanyon.net/item/eform-wordpress-form-builder/3180835)
of it on Envato. Eventually my journey as a product creator started and I have been making
products ever since.

It's been ~15 years since. Today I am not just a product creator, but a part of a broader
product team. I get to brainstorm and ideate the product, and also implement or help my
team implement it. I also listen to the active users of the product and see various use
cases and scenarios which we never thought of. Sometimes those use cases become the most
interesting features of the product.

Today I want to reflect on on my journey and talk about the most interesting thing I have
learned in the way. **Empathy**. I think this is one of the greatest quality of an
Engineering leader that can shape or break both the product and the team.

## Empathy in Engineering

We all know what empathy is. It is the ability to understand and share the feelings of
another. Let's see how it materelizes in product and engineering.

It is day one and you launch your product. You are excited and happy to see the first few
users using your product.

But then comes the feedback, sometimes brutal and harsh.

I think as product makers, it is in our reflex to to immediately discard the nay sayers.
And I think those of us who have learnt to manage this reflex, have been able to create
better and popular products.

Let's boil it down.

## Empathizing with the Customers

This I think is the greatest skill a product maker must have. Yes I know, you've spent
countless hours, blood and sweat to create the product.

But at the end of the day, this product is just not for you, but also for your customers.
You may think that a particular experience is what you want, but it may not be what your
customers want.

If you see enough feedback and reviews, then it is time to rethink and rebuild.

## Emphatizing with the Team

From my experience many great ideas and use-cases also come internally. Think about it.
Your team is building and supporting the product. In many times, they are talking with the
customers. They are also seeing the UX first-hand.

As the Engineering leader you can't have your eyes and ears all over the place. That's why
you delegate.

But as much like delegation, empathy also needs to flow towards your team. Listen to them,
ask actively for feedback, ask what they think is the best, and action on that.

While doing that, you will see that not only they care about the product, but also about
the speed at which they can deliver it.

## Actionable Behavior & Workflows

Now I don't want you to just read this and say "yeah that's all theoretical, I will keep
it in mind". I want you to think how you can implement this in your behavior and workflows

How you do it exactly, will depend on the type of work you're doing. I can only give you
some examples from my own experience.

### Always Look into Support

This is the greatest (and also most noisy) source of feedback. From here you need to
filter out real issues and use-cases.

From my experience, even if you have a great team, and even if enginners are doing
support, they can still miss obvious platform improvements that can come from it. It is
understandable. I think even if you start doing support yourself, you will also miss many
things. We have a tendency to solve problems in front of us, and then moving on, instead
of looking at the bigger picture. It happens even more, when there's a lot of tickets and
you're crunching on time.

This is where, you, the Engineering Leader come in. Make it your habit to

1. Go through the support tickets.
2. Talk regularly (maybe once a week or once a month) with the support team and ask if
   something stood out to them.
3. See if there are obvious wins you can get from a pattern of tickets.

Do this enough time and you will start seeing patterns and use-cases that you never
thought of. Even 1% of hater feedback can turn into a 10% improvement in your product. And
that is the power of empathy.

And how do we cut the noise from a lot of support tickets? Use your team and your
judgement 😉.

### Talk to the Customers

Sometimes you will find similar product makers who are also your customers. Talk to them.
Ask them what they like and what they don't like. Ask them what they think is missing.

Conversation with your high value customers often leads to the most interesting use-cases
and features.

### Write Code Along with your Team

We are engineers at the end of the day. We love to code, we love to build. This is what
keeps us going.

But as the Engineering leader, you may not be able to code as much as you want. But I
think it is important to get your hands dirty every once in a while.

But what if your time is demanding? I have a solution for that. **Code Reviews**.

Often I don't just leave review feedback, but also do small changes and improvements
myself.

If the feature is in active development and the review is for the architecture and design,
I find myself exploring hands on and creating a few POCs before giving my feedback.

I think this does two things:

1. You stay close to the codebase and the product -- You understand estimation and risk
   better.
2. You show your team that you are not just a manager, but also a fellow engineer who is
   willing to get his hands dirty and help them out.

For better or worse, I think this is a great way to **empathize with the code**.

### Talk and Sync with the Team

I don't just mean the usual slack messages and endofdays or progress reports. Discuss the
task with your team, the ins and outs. Ask what they think of a feature, how are they
enjoying (or hating) it. What they think our engineering process lacks that can make us
happy and productive.

Many internal tooling and process improvements have come from these discussions. And I
think this is a great way to empathize with your team and their work. I believe happy
shipping is better than fast shipping.

This is also the process that I like to do most, too much to be honest. I can even give
you a few concrete examples:

1. We had some e2e testing written in an outdated framework with limited license. Only one
   team member could work it and they didn't like. It was also slowing us down to write
   new tests and many of the team members were just documenting the e2e tests in an Asana
   task. We sat down and discussed the problem and it took us only a little time to come
   up with a migration plan. Now this was before the time of AI. We spent around 1 sprint
   of an engineer to migrate the tests, but in the end our shipping speed increased and
   the risk went down, down a lot.
2. Another example, that greately improved our public facing documentation. We were using
   some outdated (yet managed) documentation platform. It was slow, and no engineers liked
   it. We would write a documentation, only when we were forced to. Many documentation
   ideas came, but they were shelved because either we didn't want to touch the platform,
   or the platform didn't support the way we wanted to write it. We took a bigger ambition
   here and decided to migrate. 2 months later our shiny new platform was live. 1 year
   later every one from the engineering team has contributed to the documentation, in one
   way or another. We are happy, the business is happy and most importantly, our customers
   (and LLMs) are happy.

## Empathize with Yourself

Finally, I think it is very important to empathize with thyself. We need to know the limit
of ourselves, understand when we are stressed and running out of ideas.

As an Engineering leader, our team is looking to us as the fail-safe. We are the ones who
are expected to have the answers.

But it doesn't mean we have to pretend when we don't. I find a straight forward approach
works best. Discuss with your team, brainstorm ideas together and be confident that you
will find a solution together.
